Interpolating the ISDF Coulomb tile to arbitrary momentum: theory and results of the F‑scheme

Abstract. We want the bare-exchange tile VQ of the interpolative-separable density-fitting (ISDF) representation at momenta Q off the coarse grid, without ever forming an object carrying the full real-space dimension, and without redoing the fit at each target. Direct routes fail for instructive reasons: the fitting vectors ζq are the dual basis of an ill-conditioned frame and are not smooth in q; the divergent long-range Coulomb weight makes even the tile itself rough. The working construction — clean the tile spectrally, split the Coulomb kernel into Gaussian short- and long-range parts, interpolate only the short-range tile, and rebuild the long-range channel from phase-factored form factors — reaches 0.6% median error on physical matrix elements and 0.05 meV on exciton energies, within 1.6× of the best scheme that carries the full-grid data, at roughly 1/100 the per-target cost. Along the way, the “chaotic frame” folklore is narrowed to a precise operator-theory statement, and a dipole–quadrupole multipole alternative is ruled out quantitatively.

1The objects

ISDF compresses products of Bloch functions onto a set of nμ interpolation points rμ (centroids). For each transfer momentum q on the coarse grid, the pair density is approximated as

ψ*m,k−q(r) ψn,k(r)  ≈  Σμ  ζq,μ(r)  ψ*m,k−q(rμ)  ψn,k(rμ) (1)

and the fitting vectors solve the least-squares normal equations

Cq ζq = Zq,     Cq[μν] = Σk,mn ρ*mn,k,q(rμ)  ρmn,k,q(rν) (2)

where Cq is the nμ×nμ Gram matrix of pair densities sampled at the centroids and Zq is the centroid‑to‑full‑grid cross-Gram — the one object whose second dimension is the full grid (rtot), and the one we refuse to carry. The exchange tile is the frame-free quadratic contraction

Vq[μν] = ΣG  ζ̃*μ(q,G)  v(q+G)  ζ̃ν(q,G) (3)

with v the (slab-truncated) Coulomb kernel. Everything the BSE consumes from this tile is of the form B = M†VQM, where Mcvk(μ) are pair amplitudes of band states near the gap — this physical contraction, together with the exciton-energy shift when only the tile is swapped, is the metric everywhere below. Tile Frobenius norms turn out to be almost meaningless, which is half the story.

2Why the obvious routes fail

Two measured facts frame the problem. First, the ingredients are genuinely smooth: the lattice Fourier transforms CR, ZR decay superalgebraically (a nearsightedness statement — they are quadratic in density matrices, which decay exponentially in a gapped system), and leave-one-out interpolation of Cq is accurate at the 0.1–1% level. Second, the object one actually needs, ζq = Cq−1Zq, is not smooth: cond(C) ∼ 107–109 with a gapless spectrum, and the inverse of a short-ranged operator is long-ranged — ζR is nearly flat in R. Interpolating ζ, or anything wearing C’s eigenframe, fails.

The operator-theory statement is worth having exactly. Davis–Kahan bounds subspace rotation by ‖ΔC‖/gap; a hard rank cut placed inside a gapless spectrum has no protecting gap, so cut-edge eigenvectors rotate freely under arbitrarily smooth variation of Cq. Benzi–Boito–Razouk’s decay bounds require the spectral function to be analytic in an ellipse clearing the spectrum — a step function inside a gapless spectrum has no theorem; an analytic filter of width ε does. Measured on adjacent grid points: plain top-m subspaces of Cq are in fact smooth (principal-cosine affinity 0.975–0.9998 for every m up to 480, against random floors of 0.13–0.87); only whitened, S−1-amplified eigenframe statistics sit at the random floor — as perturbation theory requires them to. The earlier “frames are chaotic” folklore was a true measurement of the wrong object.

Observation 1 The pair-space geometry varies smoothly with q; the dual basis of the centroid point-value frame does not. Licensed objects for interpolation are frame-free quadratics (the tiles, the Grams) and analytically-filtered functions of Cq; forbidden ones are eigenvector frames, whitened sections, and anything transported along them.

But frame-freedom alone is not enough. Interpolating the raw tile fails at the 16–50% level, and — the campaign’s central surprise — spectrally cleaning the tile first barely helps (15%). The dominant q-roughness of Vq is not junk in the ill-conditioned tail; it is the near-head Coulomb weight: v(q+G) at small |q+G| varies violently across the zone (and diverges at the head), and the label “G = 0” is not even periodic across the zone boundary — the head channel winds.

3The F-scheme

Three moves, each answering one failure mode above. All coarse-side objects are built once from stored data; the per-target cost is a handful of nμ2 AXPYs plus an analytic rebuild. No solve, no eigendecomposition, no rtot object at or after interpolation.

3.1 Clean without touching Z — once per coarse point

Everything in this subsection happens at the coarse grid points qj only — each in that point’s own frame, as an offline preparation pass. Rank-cutting the stored fit at qj is a projection: with eigh(Cqj) = RqjΛqjRHqj and Pqj the projector onto its top-r eigenvectors,

ζrc(qj) = Pqj ζstored(qj)   ⇒   Vc(qj) = Πqj V(qj) Πqj,   Πqj = P*qj (4)

so the cleaned tile at each coarse point is computable from that point’s stored tile and ψ-level Gram alone — the identity was verified against an explicit refit at the 10−14 level. A smooth (Tikhonov) filter gε(λ) = λ2/(λ2+ε2) in place of the hard cut is the theory-preferred variant; it is measurably smoother in q (cleaned-tile increments are 70–90× smaller than raw-tile increments) and slightly better downstream.

Note what Eq. (4) does not do: it never compares, aligns, or transports eigenvectors between different qj. Each projector is absorbed into its own tile, and from that point on only the frame-free nμ2 objects Vc(qj) exist. That is why the scheme survives the frame chaos of §2: the map q ↦ ΠqV(q)Πq is smooth even though q ↦ (individual eigenvectors) is not — for the Tikhonov filter this is operator-function continuity outright, and for the hard cut the freely-rotating cut-edge modes carry only the physically inert weight, so their rotation barely moves the tile.

3.2 Split the kernel, interpolate only the short-range part

Following the polar electron–phonon lineage (subtract the analytic long-range part, interpolate the smooth remainder, re-add at the target), split per G-channel with a Gaussian window, K = q+G:

vLR(K) = v(K) e−K2/4α2,    vSR = v − vLR,    VSR,c(qj) = Vc(qj) − Πqj VLR(qj) Πqj (5)

The long-range channel — everything divergent, everything winding — is confined to a fixed global superset 𝒢(α) of Miller indices (those with minq|q+G|2 ≤ 4α2ln(1/εLR); 337 vectors at α = 0.3), with tail bounds at the 10−17 level. The short-range tile that remains is smooth in q and interpolates through a truncated-R Fourier stencil at the same accuracy as the ingredients. The window width has a broad optimum at α ≈ 1.5–2× the grid spacing, and the rank-cut sits in a wide inert window — neither is a delicate knob.

3.3 Carry the long-range channel as phase-factored form factors

The winding of the head is cured analytically, not interpolated through. Define, per coarse q and per G in the superset,

Fμ(q;G) = e+2πi(q+G)·sμ  ζ̃c,μ(q+G) (6)

— the exact form factor of the cleaned fitting vector with the full centroid phase e−iK·sμ factored out. At fixed Miller index these are periodic, winding-safe functions of q (adjacent-q roughness drops from 0.68 for the raw head slot to 0.31), and they interpolate componentwise. The assembled prediction at a target q0, with stencil weights wj, is

V(q0) = Σj wj  VSR,c(qj)  +  ΣG∈𝒢  vLR(q0+G)  e−iΔφF*μ Fν​(q0;G) (7)

with the interpolated form factors re-dressed by the exact analytic phases. Storage for the whole long-range channel is nμ×337 per coarse point — 124 MB against the 17 GB of Zq at the same fixture.

3.4 Where each operation lives: grid qj versus target Q

Collecting the pipeline, because the division of labor is the whole point:

Offline, once per coarse grid point qj (all heavy linear algebra lives here): one eigh(Cqj) → Πqj; the cleaned split tile VSR,c(qj) via Eqs. (4)–(5); the form-factor block F(qj;G) via Eq. (6). These are stored: one nμ2 tile and one nμ×|𝒢| block per coarse point.

At an arbitrary target Q (cheap, no linear algebra): stencil weights wj(Q) come from truncated-R trigonometric interpolation — the same weights for every matrix element, fixed by the coarse grid geometry and Q alone. Then

VSR(Q) = Σj wj(Q) VSR,c(qj),     F(Q;G) = Σj wj(Q) F(qj;G) (7′)

and Eq. (7) assembles V(Q) from these plus the closed-form vLR(Q+G) and centroid phases e−i(Q+G)·sμ, which are evaluated analytically at Q — they are known functions, not interpolants. There is no CQ, no eigendecomposition, no projector, and no fit at the target; the eigendecompositions of §3.1 relate to the interpolation only by having prepared, at each coarse point separately, the smooth objects that the stencil then combines. (The one exception in the results table is diagnostic row C, which deliberately uses the target’s own stored long-range channel to bound what any LR treatment could achieve — the honest F rows do not.)

Observation 2 The split, not the cleaning, is the load-bearing move. Cleaning alone (row B) leaves 15% error — indistinguishable from raw — because the tile’s roughness is Coulomb-weight variation near the head, not ill-conditioned junk. Conversely, the junk really is physically inert: deleting 90% of the tile’s Frobenius norm moves the physical block by 8×10−4 and exciton energies by 0.01 meV.
Observation 3 Once the winding phase is carried analytically, the exact form factors interpolate essentially at the exact-LR ceiling (F ≈ C at matched α). Every Taylor or moment compression of them — dipole, quadrupole — loses an order of magnitude, and the multipole hierarchy inverts (monopole-only beats monopole+dipole+quadrupole): the ISDF ζ envelopes are not compact, so literal polynomial-weighted moments are dominated by delocalized tails. On a slab, the pure-3D multipole expansion additionally fails in the out-of-plane direction (73% error in the |Gz| = 1 channels, which carry 19% of the long-range weight); per-Gz treatment is mandatory.

Path smoothness — the practically relevant off-grid diagnostic — was tested along Γ→(1/6,0,0): the F trajectories of physical matrix elements and low eigenvalues are smooth (normalized second differences 3.6–4.5×10−2, slightly better than the ingredient scheme), connecting the on-grid endpoints without kinks.

5Standing verdict

  • The no-full-grid constraint is met: 0.59–0.62% median physical error / 0.045 meV excitons, within 1.6× of the Z-carrying scheme, at ∼1/100 the per-target cost and ∼1/70 the storage. The short-range side already sits at the ceiling; the whole remaining gap is long-range-channel interpolation.
  • Production default remains the per-Q refit (exact, no uncontrolled error); the F-scheme is the designated interpolation candidate, pending an off-grid ground truth in the same convergence class.
  • Do not re-attempt: eigenvector-frame transport (lawless on a gapless spectrum — that is a theorem, not a folklore); literal multipole compression of the long-range channels; band windows that split Kramers doublets (no smooth gauge exists even in principle); pure-3D multipoles on slabs.
